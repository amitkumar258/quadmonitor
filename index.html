What is the Quad up to?

Tracking the initiatives, investments, and impact of the Quad

Data as of 15 July 2026

The Quad is often criticised for being dormant, even declared dead, for lapsing into summitry, and for drifting toward soft, non-security issues. Sceptics argue it has done too little to mount a credible challenge or alternative to China, falling short of the objectives it was founded to pursue.

This dashboard is an attempt to cut through that debate using empirical data. It maps every Quad initiative across six summit cycles, tracking where the grouping cooperates, what forms that cooperation takes, how much capital is committed and how little is verifiably disbursed, and how each initiative has performed against its stated goals. Every figure is drawn from official government sources.

Cooperation by Domain

Strategic Portfolio and Scope

  • Thirty-seven initiatives across six summit cycles (2021 to 2026) mark a shift from a narrow maritime-security consultation to a broad programme spanning security, climate, infrastructure, health, and education.
  • Security leads with twenty-three initiatives across ten sub-domains, five in maritime and seven in critical and emerging technology, confirming the Quad has dispersed rather than concentrated its hard-power ambitions.
  • Climate (8) is the most ambitious public-goods agenda, spanning green shipping, clean hydrogen, ocean research, climate information services, and supply-chain diversification.
  • Infrastructure (5) offers a credible alternative to coercive connectivity financing and includes the five-star Cable Connectivity programme.
  • Health (3) has produced the most measurable human-impact initiative, the Cancer Moonshot, while Education and Exchange (1) remains nascent but strategically significant.

Cooperation by Type

Note: many initiatives have more than one cooperation type.

Cooperation Patterns

  • Capacity Building dominates with sixteen instances, nearly half the portfolio, reflecting a preference for institution-building over binding commitments or joint enforcement.
  • Interoperability (12) is equally central, reflecting sustained investment in aligning the procedures, standards, and systems of four sovereign partners who share no formal alliance obligations.
  • Resilience (4) and Standard Setting (6) signal an ambition to shape the region's technical and regulatory architecture through influence rather than mandate.
  • Data Sharing (5) and Monitoring (4) prioritise shared situational awareness over collective enforcement.
  • Supply Chain Resilience (7) has gained significant momentum, driven by three dedicated semiconductor initiatives alongside broader infrastructure and trade programmes, reflecting the Quad's deepening focus on strategic technology resilience.

Important Caveats First, the disaster relief responses for Myanmar, Papua New Guinea, and Vietnam are one-off deployments under the Humanitarian Assistance and Disaster Relief Mechanism rather than standing initiatives. Second, the Quality Infrastructure and Critical Minerals figures are aspirational pledges and mobilisation targets, not committed spending. Funding Architecture and Scale

  • The disclosed picture splits into three tiers, and the gap between them is itself the finding. The Quad's largest numbers are its softest commitments.
  • Pledges and mobilisation targets total roughly 70 billion dollars: the over 50 billion dollar Quality Infrastructure pledge and the up to 20 billion dollar Critical Minerals target. Neither is yet backed by committed or allocated funding.
  • Loan facilities form a second tier of roughly 4.3 billion dollars, dominated by Japan's 3.3 billion dollar JICA COVID-19 Crisis Response Emergency Support Loan programme and 800 million dollars in US DFC facilities.
  • Aid and grants total roughly 9.9 billion dollars, but this figure is dominated by COVID-era multilateral health flows: COVAX (5.6 billion), Last-Mile delivery (2 billion), and CEPI (524 million) account for over 8.1 billion of it.
  • Setting those three aside, discrete bilateral grants and aid — the money attributable to a single member and initiative — come to roughly 1.75 billion dollars across the entire portfolio, led by Australia's vaccine-era health flows.
  • The Quad's confirmed cash footprint therefore remains a small fraction of its headline figures.

Funding by Country, Domain, and Delivery

  • Australia leads individual member commitments at just over 1 billion dollars, including its 407 million dollar standing Pacific vaccine commitment folded into the Quad Vaccine Partnership. The United States follows at around 201 million dollars, then Japan at around 190 million dollars.
  • India's direct disclosed contributions stand at around 20 million dollars, lifted by its 17.5 million dollar Cancer Moonshot commitment. Its in-kind contributions — notably vaccine manufacturing through Biological E. — remain a significant non-cash input that the dollar figures understate.
  • Collectively attributed money — commitments made by all four members jointly — comes to roughly 8.4 billion dollars, almost all of it COVID-era multilateral health aid.
  • By domain, Health dominates at roughly 9.5 billion dollars. Security follows at around 226 million dollars, then Climate (~156 million), Infrastructure (~148 million), and Education and Exchange (~10.5 million).
  • COVAX (5.6 billion), Last-Mile delivery (2 billion), and CEPI (524 million) are confirmed multilateral aid contributions, not aspirational pledges: government sources confirm these funds were released and disbursed.
  • Disbursement reporting remains the biggest transparency gap. Where audits exist — the US DFC loan to Biological E., Japan's JICA emergency loans — they confirm delivery, but most commitments still lack public disbursement data, making independent value-for-money assessment difficult.
